2. Murphy and Wyness. (2023). Testing Means-Tested Aid. JOLE.
  • Question: Can financial aid be used to help equalize the outcomes of students once they are enrolled in college?
  • Broader Question: Financial aids → academic outcomes
  • Policy: Institutional Financial Aid (IFA) - unexpected, means-test, no need to apply
  • Outcomes: first year completion rate, course scores, good degree (1st, 2:1) or not
  • Data Source: admin data: 9 English universities
  • Sample: students entering college between 2006/7 and 2009/10 (22,770 students)
  • Identification:
    1) OLS (with the “heavily parameterized regression design” - controling a smooth function of parental income to separate out the effect at discontinuities of the IFA) and
    2) IV (eligibility → amount of aid → outcomes)
  • Conclusion: Each £1,000 of IFA during the first year of college
    1) increases the chances of completing that year by 1.4 percentage points,
    2) improves test scores by 0.059σ, and
    3) increases the chances of graduating with a good-degree by 3.4 percentage points.
  • Heterogeneity: High ability and lowincome students benefit the most.
3. Denning et al. (2019). ProPelled: The Effects of Grants on Graduation, Earnings, and Welfare. AEJ: Applied Economics.
  • Question: How does eligibility for additional grant aid provided to low-income college students affect their academic and labor market outcomes?
  • Broader Question: Financial assistance → academic and labor market outcomes
  • Policy: Pell Grant aid eligible students (family income lower than some threshold) are more likely to receive an additional aid of about $700.
  • Outcomes:
    1) short-run: credit hours attempted, persistence/2nd year enrolment, GPA
    2) long-run: graduation time, earnings afterwards
  • Data Source: admin data from Texas Higher Education Coordinating Board (THECB) and Texas Workforce Commission (TWC)
  • Sample: first-time-in-college and returning students who enrolled in a public four-year institution in Texas between 2008 and 2011.
  • Identification: Fuzzy RD
  • Conclusion:
    1) Eligibility for additional grant aid significantly increases degree completion and later earnings of first-time four-year university students.
    2) The government should fully recover the increase in grant expenditures within 10 years.
