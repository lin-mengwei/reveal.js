1-2. Esther Duflo (2004), AER.
  • Question: How will school construction improve schooling and labor market outcomes?
  • Broader Question: infrastructure → human capital formation
  • Policy change: INPRES program: School constructions in 1973/74 and 1978/79 in Indonesia
  • Data: Cross-sectional.
    individual level: intercensal survey of Indonesia 1995 (N = 152,989);
    district-level school building data
  • Sample: Men born between 1950 and 1972
  • Identification : Difference in Difference
    (High-level region - Low-level region) - (More-exposed cohort - Less-exposed cohort)
    1) Schooling: \[ S_{ijk} = c_1 + \alpha_{1j} + \beta_{1k} + (P_jT_i)\gamma_1 + (\mathbf{C}_jT_i)\delta_1 + \varepsilon_{ijk} \] 2) Wage: \[ y_{ijk} = c_1 + \alpha_{1j} + \beta_{1k} + (P_jT_i)\gamma_1 + (\mathbf{C}_jT_i)\delta_1 + \varepsilon_{ijk} \]
  • Conclusion: For each primary school constructed for every 1,000 children, there is
    1) an increase in years of education ranging from 0.12 to 0.19 on average, and
    2) a rise in wages from 1.5 to 2.7 percent.
